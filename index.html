The statistical methods or techniques are applicable only when some data are available, irrespective of the method of data collection. The data can be quantitative as well as qualitative. If the data are qualitative, they are quantified by using techniques like ranking, scoring, scaling, or coding, etc. The data are collected either by experiments or by survey methods (directly or indirectly) and they are tabulated and analyzed statistically. Whatever may be the resulting value(s) obtained from analysis, proper and correct inferences have to be drawn from these numerical values. These inferences lead to a final decision.

Functions of Statistics

On the basis of these ideas, we can broadly give the following functions of statistics:

  • (i) Collection of data
  • (ii) Tabulation of data
  • (iii) Analysis of data
  • (iv) Interpretation of results

Detailed Functions

Collection of Data

Once it is decided what type of study is to be made, it becomes necessary to collect information about the concerned study, mostly in the form of data. For this, information has to be collected from certain individuals directly or indirectly. Such a technique is known as **survey method**, commonly used in social sciences. Another way of collecting data is by **experimentation**, used in agriculture, biology, medical science, etc.

Types of Data

  • **Primary Data:** The data which are collected from the units or individual respondents directly for the purpose of a certain study or information.
  • **Secondary Data:** The data which had been collected by certain people or agency and statistically treated. This information is then used again from records, processed and statistically analyzed to extract some information for other purposes.

Processing of Data (Primary Data Scrutiny)

Before tabulation of primary data, it should be scrutinized for completeness, consistency, accuracy, and editing:

  • **Completeness:** If important information is missing, the informant must be contacted again. If completion is impossible, the questionnaire should be discarded or revised.
  • **Accuracy:** This is of vital importance. If data are inaccurate, the conclusions drawn from it have no relevance or reliability.
  • **Editing:** To maintain **homogeneity**, information sheets are checked to ensure the unit of information or measurement is the same in all schedules (e.g., converting all income figures to monthly or annual units).

Tabulation of Data

The information collected through an enquiry or experiment may be presented in the form of tables or graphs. These tables enable us to come to some conclusion and make certain comparisons.

Analysis of Data

As given by Zacks, "one of the most important objectives in the primary stage of statistical analysis is to process the observed data and transform it to a form most suitable for decision making." This processing generally reduces the size of the original sets of sample values to a relatively small number of statistics, ideally losing no information relevant to the decision process.

Interpretation of Data

Once the data have been analyzed, the main job consists of attaching physical meaning and giving interpretation to the numerical results useful in real life. This interpretation must be true in its meaning and sense. The quality of interpretation depends on the experience and insight of the person. No pre-conceived ideas should be forced onto the results, and no attempts should be made to draw more inferences than the results are actually liable to.

Why Statistics is Crucial for Cybersecurity üõ°Ô∏è

Statistics plays a crucial role in various aspects of cybersecurity, allowing for the analysis of high-volume, real-time data to detect threats and predict risks. This science-based approach enables the development of robust, validated predictive models.

Here are some key applications:

  1. Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS): Statistical methods analyze network traffic patterns and identify **anomalies** that may indicate a security breach. Techniques like clustering and classification help distinguish between normal and suspicious activities.
  2. Risk Assessment: Statistical models help in **quantifying risks** associated with different cyber threats, allowing organizations to prioritize security measures based on likelihood and impact.
  3. Malware Analysis: Statistical techniques analyze malware behavior and characteristics, facilitating the identification of new strains and helping in the development of detection signatures.
  4. Fraud Detection: In financial transactions and online activities, statistical algorithms can identify unusual patterns that may indicate fraudulent behavior, such as sudden spikes in transaction amounts or frequency.
  5. Predictive Analytics: By analyzing historical data, statistical models can **predict potential future attacks** or vulnerabilities, enabling proactive measures to be taken.
  6. Data Privacy: Techniques such as **differential privacy** use statistical methods to anonymize data while still allowing for meaningful analysis, helping organizations comply with data protection regulations.
  7. Performance Monitoring: Statistics are used to monitor the performance of security systems, analyzing metrics like false positives and false negatives to improve detection mechanisms.
  8. Vulnerability Assessment: Statistical analysis can help in evaluating the **likelihood of vulnerabilities being exploited** based on historical data and threat intelligence.
  9. Security Compliance: Organizations often use statistical sampling methods to **audit compliance** with security policies and standards, ensuring that controls are effective.
  10. User Behavior Analytics (UBA): By applying statistical analysis to user behavior, organizations can establish baselines and **detect deviations** that may indicate compromised accounts or insider threats.

Statistical Modeling in Action (Network Traffic)

In order to respond quickly to a cyber security attack, organizations need to analyze high-volumes of traffic data and detect anomalies in real time. For example, some work using statistical techniques such as **sequential hypothesis testing** has been shown to be possible in operational networks transferring terabytes of data daily.

The basic idea in some models is to treat accesses to local IP addresses as a **random walk** on one of two stochastic processes (benign or malicious). While this work is promising, new mathematical models are needed to determine whether a coordinated attack from a set of remote addresses is taking place, especially as networks increase in bandwidth.

Furthermore, network traffic is often modeled as a **Poisson process**, but many studies show that simple Poisson models do not hold for real Internet traffic, which often exhibits more complex statistical properties like **self-similarity** in local area networks.
